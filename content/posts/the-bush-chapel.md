The first thing you hear is the piano.

Pete McClean has been playing for church for seventy-two years. He taught himself at sixteen. Today, somewhere in the foothills of the Dandenong Ranges in Victoria, Australia, he plays the same hymns he was playing when his great-grandchildren were born — and a small camera off to one side captures it for The Bush Chapel.

This is not a megachurch. It is not a viral creator account. There are no jump cuts, no overlay captions begging you to like and subscribe, no urgency. There is a piano, a Bible reading, a few words of reflection, and a closing prayer. The whole thing takes fifteen to twenty minutes. The team behind it would rather you put the kettle on first.

> *"Grab yourself a cuppa and join us in the quiet of the Australian bush."*

That's the welcome on [thebushchapel.com](https://thebushchapel.com). Behind it is a small voluntary team led by **Rev Dr Ted Woods** — a Bible teacher with a doctorate in theology, two decades lecturing in Old Testament at the Bible College of Victoria, thirteen years pastoring Upwey Baptist Church, and a credit on the rewritten Tyndale Commentary on Deuteronomy. He preaches like someone who has spent a lifetime in the text, because he has.

He is not the founder, though. That credit belongs to his daughter **Emma Woods**, a former creative director with eighteen years in church design, who started The Bush Chapel for "Christian individuals close to us who had not been able to attend their local church." Bedridden friends. Elderly relatives in nursing homes. People in country parishes too far from a Sunday service. People in hospital. The audience is the part of the church the algorithm has never been built for.

## A liturgy you can follow from bed

The format is deliberately the same every week: a hymn at the piano, a lectionary reading, a short reflection, prayer, another hymn, a benediction. Each service has a printable transcript, so you can follow along in bed if your eyes are tired or your hearing is going. The recent calendar reads like the calendar of a small parish anywhere — *Easter 2A — Fullness of Joy*, *Lent 4A — The Man Born Blind and Lenten Seeing*, *Advent 1A — Gladness in God's House*. Anyone who has ever flipped through a lectionary will recognise the rhythm immediately.

The team is small and ecumenical. **Jenny Woods**, who organises the community side, was a nurse in Zambia and Australia and comes from a family long woven into mission, ministry, and nursing. **John Waterson**, the Anglican on the team, studied theology at Durham and Whitley before — in the most Australian career pivot imaginable — ending up making wine at Chateau Yering in the Yarra Valley. And then there's Pete and his piano, of course. Each service is acknowledged on Wurundjeri Country.

## The opposite of an algorithm

There is something quietly subversive about all of this.

Algorithmic Faith exists because the platforms are reshaping how the gospel travels — what gets made, who sees it, what wins. Most of the creators we'll feature on this site are wrestling with the algorithm in some way: chasing it, gaming it, refusing it, trying to be honest under it. The Bush Chapel is doing something different. It is using YouTube the way a small parish uses a notice board. Not to grow, not to scale, not to monetise. Just to make sure that the people who can't be in the room on Sunday morning can still hear the piano.

> *"Whether you're joining us from the city or the country, you're welcome here, just as you are."*

That sentence will not trend. It will not be A/B tested into a thumbnail. It is the opposite of how the platform wants you to write. And maybe that is exactly why The Bush Chapel deserves to be the first creator we spotlight on this site — because it is the kind of ministry the algorithm was never meant to find, and that we hope it does anyway.

Make yourself a cuppa first.
