There's a new kind of testimony happening on TikTok, and it doesn't look anything like what you'd see in a church service.

## The 60-second sermon

Faith creators on TikTok have figured out something that traditional churches have struggled with for years — how to get to the point.

When you've got 60 seconds (or less), you can't meander. You can't do a 10-minute introduction. You have to lead with the hook, deliver the message, and land the ending. Every second counts.

And honestly? Some of these clips hit harder than hour-long sermons.

## Who's doing it well

A few creators worth watching:

- **@praisethelord** — Raw, unfiltered testimonies set to worship music. Simple formula, massive engagement.
- **@jackiehillperry** — Theological depth in short-form. She doesn't compromise the message to fit the format.
- **@pastormark** — Sermon clips cut for maximum impact. The editing is tight and the hooks are strong.

## The algorithm factor

TikTok's algorithm is different from YouTube's. Here's what matters:

- **Completion rate** — If people watch your full video, TikTok pushes it. Short + compelling = more reach.
- **Shares over likes** — A video someone shares to their story or sends to a friend gets way more algorithmic boost than a simple like.
- **Trends and sounds** — Faith creators who use trending audio get discovered by people who'd never search for faith content. That's the real power.

## The tension

Here's what I keep thinking about: does the format shape the message? When you optimise for completion rate and shares, are you actually sharing the gospel — or are you creating content that happens to have faith-adjacent themes?

I don't think it's either/or. But it's worth being honest about the tension.

The best TikTok faith creators I've seen are the ones who use the platform's rules without letting those rules define their message. That's the line to walk.
